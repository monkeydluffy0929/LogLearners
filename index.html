INTRODUCTION

When and who invented it?

The concept of logarithms dates back to the early 17th century, with the Scottish mathematician John Napier and the Swiss mathematician Jost Bürgi. Napier introduced the concept in his work "Mirifici Logarithmorum Canonis Descriptio" in 1614. Logarithms were initially devised as a computational aid to simplify multiplication and division, transforming them into addition and subtraction.

Where does the word Logarithms comes from?

The term "logarithm" is derived from the Greek words: "logos," meaning ratio or word, and "arithmos," meaning number. When these two words combine, "logarithm" essentially refers to a ratio number or a number representing a ratio.

Real life application

The logarithmic scale can be applied in real-life scenarios such as navigation, astronomy, and other fields requiring extensive computations.

CONCEPT OF LOGARITHMS

Logarithms are a mathematical concept that represents the inverse operation of exponentiation. In simple terms, logarithms help us solve exponential equations by finding the exponent to which a specific base must be raised to obtain a given number.

What are Logarithms?

A logarithm is defined as the power to which a number must be raised to get some other values. It is the most convenient way to express large numbers. A logarithm has various important properties that prove multiplication and division of logarithms can also be written in the form of logarithm of addition and subtraction. “The logarithm of a positive real number a with respect to base b, a positive real number not equal to 1[nb 1], is the exponent by which b must be raised to yield a”.

LOGARITHMIC LAW AND PROPERTIES

There are a number of rules known as the laws of logarithms. These allow expressions involving logarithms to be rewritten in a variety of different ways. The laws apply to logarithms of any base but the same base must be used throughout a calculation.

The Laws of Logarithms

The three main laws are stated here:

First Law

log A + log B = log AB

This law tells us how to add two logarithms together. Adding log A and log B results in the logarithm of the product of A and B, that is log AB.

For example, we can write log10 5 + log10 4 = log10(5 × 4) = log10 20

Second Law

log A − log B = log A/B

So, subtracting log B from log A results in log A/B.

For example, we can write loge 12 − loge 2 to loge 12/2 = loge 6

Third Law

log An = n log A

So, for example, log10 53 = 3 log10 5

You should verify this by evaluating both sides separately on your calculator.

Two other important results are log 1 = 0, logm m = 1

The logarithm of 1 to any base is always 0, and the logarithm of a number to the same base is always 1. In particular, log10 10 = 1, and loge e = 1

Multiplication

What is the rule when you multiply two values with the same base together (x3 * x3)? The rule is that you keep the base and add the exponents. Well, remember that logarithms are exponents, and when you multiply, you're going to add the logarithms.

The log of a product is the sum of the logs.

logaxy = loga + loga y

Example:

loga(5y) = loga(y)

↓

loga(5) = loga(y)

Division

The rule when you divide two values with the same base is to subtract the exponents. Therefore, the rule for division is to subtract the logarithms.

The log of a quotient is the difference of the logs.

loga (x/y) = loga x - loga y

Example:

log4(x3) - log4(y)

↓

log4(x3/y)

Raising to a power

When you raise a quantity to a power, the rule is that you multiply the exponents together. In this case, one of the exponents will be the log, and the other exponent will be the power you're raising the quantity to.

The exponent on the argument is the coefficient of the log.

loga xr = r × loga x

Example:

log2(x3) = 3 × log2(x)

↓

3 log2(x)

Formula's for solving logarithmic word problems

Exponential Growth

P(t) = P0 (1 - r)t

The process that increases quantity over time at an ever-increasing rate

Exponential Decay

P(t) = P0 (1 - r)t

The process of reducing an amount by a consistent percentage rate over a period of time

Half-life of a Substance

P(t) = P0 (1/2)t/k

It is the period of time needed for a substance to break down or decay into halves.

Strategies for solving logarithmic word problems

Here are strategies for solving equations that contain exponents from math.libretexts.org.(1) If the coefficient, base, and exponent are all known, we only need to evaluate the expression for coefficient(base) exponent to evaluate its value.(2) If the variable is the coefficient, evaluate the expression for (base) exponent. Then it becomes a linear equation which we solve by dividing to isolate the variable.(3) If the variable is in the exponent, use logarithms to solve the equation.(4) If the variable is not in the exponent, but is in the base, use roots to solve the equation.

Example 1

Suppose that you test apple juice and find that the hydrogen ion concentration is [H+] = 0.0003. Find the pH value and determine whether the juice is basic or acidic?

Solution:

pH = -log[H+]

pH = −log[0.0003]

pH = 3.52287874528…

Because the value is less than 7, it means that the apple juice is acidic
